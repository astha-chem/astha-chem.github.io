Thesis Research

A Device to Measure Zeta Potential at High Ionic Strength

We have developed a device to measure zeta potential up to saturated salt conditions, applicable to polymeric particles, oil emulsions and biological cells. Our experiments and Monte-Carlo simulations provide insight into the angstrom scale electrical double layer close to saturated salt conditions. We are currently working on characterizing concentration-gradient driven, diffusiophoresis flows at high ionic strength, where they are often thought to be negligible but our measurements have clearly indicated to the contrary.

Material exchange in mineral replacement reactions

Mineral replacement reactions such as petrification of wood are driven by fluid flows which result in porosity in the starting mineral. We are trying to understand and control these flows to control the porosity generated, in collaboration with Halliburton. A recent poster on this topic can be accessed here.

Zeta potential non-uniformity of active microparticles

We measured the zeta potential of each end of active platinum/polystyrene Janus particles using video microscopy rotational electrophoresis, in order to estimate the electrostatic contribution to their interaction with a wall. Currently we are using microfluidics to understand their behavior in hydrogen peroxide fuel gradients. Our paper on this subject can be found here.
